## Why We Chose This Book

September's "back to school" energy called for something academic, and what better than the novel that essentially invented the "dark academia" aesthetic? Several members had read it in college; revisiting it years later proved illuminating.

<ReadingNote title="A Note on Structure">
Tartt tells us who dies on the first page. The suspense isn't "what happened" but "how did we get here?" This inverted mystery structure mirrors Greek tragedy, where audiences knew the myths.
</ReadingNote>

## Discussion Highlights

Our discussion ranged from literary analysis to guilty pleasures:

1. **Richard as Narrator** - How reliable is he? How much does he hide?
2. **The Bacchanal** - Aesthetic transcendence or self-indulgent nonsense?
3. **Julian Morrow** - Inspiring teacher or dangerous manipulator?
4. **The Greek Ideal** - What the characters pursue vs. what they find
5. **Class Performance** - Richard's reinvention and who "belongs"

## Reading Companion

### The Greek Students

- **Richard Papen** - Our narrator, a working-class Californian seeking belonging
- **Henry Winter** - The brilliant, inscrutable leader
- **Bunny Corcoran** - The boisterous liability
- **Francis Abernathy** - The aesthete with family money
- **Charles & Camilla Macaulay** - The beautiful twins

### Greek Concepts to Know

- **Dionysian vs. Apollonian** - Chaos and order, intoxication and reason
- **Hamartia** - The tragic flaw
- **Hubris** - Pride that invites destruction
- **Catharsis** - Emotional purging through tragedy

<Spoiler>
The aftermath of Bunny's death is where Tartt really shines. The slow dissolution of the group—Henry's isolation, Charles's alcoholism, Francis's desperation—shows that the true tragedy isn't the murder but its psychic aftermath. They don't get away with it; they simply don't get caught.
</Spoiler>
