## About This Selection

*The Remains of the Day* was our July 2024 selection—a quiet summer read that left us all unexpectedly devastated. Kazuo Ishiguro's Booker Prize winner examines dignity, duty, and the costs of emotional repression.

<BookQuote author="Kazuo Ishiguro">
What is the point of worrying oneself too much about what one could or could not have done to control the course one's life took?
</BookQuote>

## Why We Chose This Book

Summer called for something shorter after our June epic (*Pachinko*). At just 258 pages, *Remains* seemed manageable—but its emotional weight was anything but light. Ishiguro packs more into restraint than most authors do into explosion.

<ReadingNote title="The Unreliable Butler">
Stevens is one of literature's great unreliable narrators—not because he lies, but because he cannot see himself clearly. Watch for the gap between what he says and what he reveals inadvertently.
</ReadingNote>

## Discussion Highlights

July's meeting was surprisingly emotional for a book about a butler:

1. **Stevens's Definition of Dignity** - Noble ideal or self-delusion?
2. **Miss Kenton** - What might have been
3. **Lord Darlington** - Culpability and the "just following orders" defense
4. **The English Landscape** - Ishiguro's use of setting as character
5. **The Final Scene** - That pier. That sunset. Those tears (ours).

### Historical Context

The novel is set in 1956 but flashes back to the 1920s and 30s. Understanding British appeasement politics—the attempts to negotiate with Nazi Germany—adds crucial context to Lord Darlington's actions.

<Spoiler>
The tragedy crystallizes in Stevens's final reflection: he has given his best years to a man who was, at best, a fool and, at worst, a Nazi sympathizer. His "dignity" was actually a mechanism for avoiding moral responsibility. When he finally acknowledges his feelings for Miss Kenton—to himself, at sunset, on a pier—it's too late. She's married. His life is spent.
</Spoiler>

## Film Adaptation

The 1993 Merchant Ivory film starring Anthony Hopkins and Emma Thompson is widely considered one of the best literary adaptations ever made. We held an optional movie night after our discussion.
